Community Solar

Overview Benefits Models Locations Available Joining Community Solar

Overview

According to the U.S. Department of Energy community solar is defined "as any solar project or purchasing program, within a geographic area, in which the benefits flow to multiple customers such as individuals, businesses, nonprofits, and other groups."

People who buy or subscribe to community solar own a portion of solar energy produced at an off-site array. These customers receive credit on their electricity bill for the electricity produced by their portion of the system. Community solar is the best option for people who are renting their home or for people who can't install panels on their house due to cost or compatibility.

Benefits

There are many benefits to community solar.

  • Houses that cannot support solar panels can still run on solar energy through community solar.
  • Solar panels can sometimes damage house roofs and the panels themselves can be damaged. Through community solar homeowners aren't responsible for maintenance.
  • Solar panels might decrease the aesthetics of a home. Homes using community solar don't have these problems.
  • Community solar can save homeowners money on their electricity bills as solar energy is pretty cheap to generate and many cities provide credits for homes using solar energy

Models

When you decided that community solar will work for you and your house, you will sign a contract with the provider. The contract will contain important information, including contract price, term length, additional charges, renewal, dispute resolution, insurance, and system maintenance. The contract may be short term or long term. There are three main types of community solar subscriptions:

  1. Fixed rate (kWh): Each kilowatt-hour (kWh) purchased from the organization is at a fixed rate and will never change for the duration of your contract.
  2. Escalating rate (kWh): Each kilowatt-hour (kWh) purchased from the organization starts out with a fixed rate. The cost will then increase at a fixed rate each year.
  3. Percentage discount (kWh): Each kilowatt-hour (kWh) purchased from the organization costs a fixed percentage below the cost of utility energy. So, if the cost of utility energy increases over time, the cost of each kilowatt-hour also increases.

Always make sure you clearly understand the terms of the contract before signing it. Ask your provider if any questions arise. It is important to understand the contract.

Locations Available

Community solar is currently found in 43 states and the District of Columbia as of 2021. Only 22 states of these 43 states have legislation that encourages community solar.

Joining Community Solar

You can find community solar programs in your state using this spreadsheet that contains recent information about programs open for enrollment. You can also contact your local utility service or search online for community solar programs in your area. The procedure for joining these programs varies from state to state and depends on the programs open for enrollment.
